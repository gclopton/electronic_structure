> [!question]
> Draw and list the symmetry operations and corresponding elements for a water molecule.


![[Screenshot 2025-06-06 at 4.53.23 PM.png]]




Water (H₂O) has four symmetry **operations** (and the corresponding geometric **elements**) that leave the molecule indistinguishable:

1. **E — identity**  
    The do-nothing operation, always present.
    
2. **C₂ (two-fold rotation axis)**  
    An axis perpendicular to the molecular plane and passing through the oxygen atom. A 180 ° rotation about this axis exchanges the two hydrogens.
    
3. **σᵥ (molecular plane)**  
    The plane that contains all three atoms—the H–O–H “V”. Reflecting through this plane leaves every atom in place.
    
4. **σᵥ′ (vertical plane perpendicular to the molecular plane)**  
    A plane that also contains the C₂ axis but bisects the H–O–H angle. Reflection through it swaps the two hydrogens while fixing the oxygen.
    

Together, **E, C₂, σᵥ, and σᵥ′** constitute the complete set of symmetry operations for a water molecule.


> [!question]
> List the symmetry operations and corresponding elements for pyridine.


### Symmetry operations and elements for pyridine (point group **C₂v**)

|Operation|Geometric element|What the operation does in pyridine|
|---|---|---|
|**E** (identity)|–|Leaves every atom unchanged. Always present.|
|**C₂** (180 ° rotation)|A two-fold axis lying **in the ring plane**, passing through the N atom, the ring centroid, and the carbon directly opposite N (C-4).|Rotating 180 ° about this axis exchanges C-2 ↔ C-6 and C-3 ↔ C-5, while N and C-4 remain in place.|
|**σᵥ**|The plane of the molecule (the aromatic ring itself). This plane **contains** the C₂ axis.|Reflection leaves every atom exactly where it is, because the molecule is flat.|
|**σᵥ′**|A plane **perpendicular to the ring**, standing upright through the C₂ axis; it bisects the N–C-4 line and the ring centroid.|Reflection swaps C-2 ↔ C-6 and C-3 ↔ C-5, leaving N and C-4 fixed.|

Together, these four symmetry operations—**E, C₂, σᵥ, σᵥ′**—constitute the complete symmetry set for pyridine and define its **C₂v** point group.


> [!NOTE]
> Many molecules have this set of symmetry elements, so it is convenient to classify them all under one name, the set of symmetry operations is called the $\mathrm{C}_{2 \mathrm{v}}$ point group, but more about this nomenclature later.

An inversion center is a point in space-usually taken as the origin of a coordinate system-through which every atom (or any point in an object) is mapped to an equivalent point directly opposite it by the operation

$$
(x, y, z) \longrightarrow(-x,-y,-z)
$$


That is, you draw a line from the atom through the inversion center and continue an equal distance on the other side; if the object looks identical after every point is so mapped, it possesses an inversion center.
- Operation: inversion (denoted 1 )
- Geometric element: the single point itself (the "center of symmetry")
- Key consequence: any atom at ( $x, y, z$ ) must have an identical partner at ( $-x,-y,-z$ ); atoms located exactly at the center remain fixed.

Molecules that contain an inversion center belong to point groups that include the $i$ operation-for example, benzene in the $D_{6h}$ point group or ethane in $D_{2h}$.

> [!question]
> Can a molecule $AB_{3}$ have an inversion center?

No. Say that the inversion center is located at A. In this case, the surrounding atoms are present in odd numbers (3), so this cannot possibly be an inversion center.
